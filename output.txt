3.0 Overview
This chapter outlines the methodology used to develop a plagiarism detection system for Meru university of Science and Technology at the school of computing and informatics. The proposed system will aim to bridge the gap identified during literature review. The chapter outlines the manner in which the study will be conducted. The key components discussed include; research design, population and sampling, data collection methods, system development methodology, system architecture and tools, data analysis methods, System testing and evaluation and ethical consideration.

3.1 Research Design
A research design provides the overall framework that guides how data is collected, analyzed, and interpreted in order to address a research problem. It serves as a structured blueprint that ensures research activities are systematically organized and aligned with the objectives of the study. In information systems and computing research, the research design must also support the development and evaluation of technological artifacts that address real-world problems (Gregor & Hevner, 2023; Peffers et al., 2022). Research designs are generally categorized into four major approaches: case study, experimental research, survey research, and design science research, each serving different research purposes depending on the nature of the study (Kitchenham & Charters, 2022; Peffers et al., 2022).
Case study research focuses on the detailed investigation of a phenomenon within its real-life context, often aiming to develop a deep contextual understanding of existing practices rather than creating a technological solution. This approach is commonly used in information systems research to analyze organizational processes, system usage patterns, or institutional workflows. While case studies can provide valuable insights into how plagiarism detection processes are currently handled within educational institutions, the approach mainly emphasizes descriptive analysis rather than the structured development of new technological artifacts (Ridder, 2022; Kitchenham & Charters, 2022). Consequently, although a case study could help understand plagiarism management practices within a university environment, it would not adequately support the systematic development of a functional plagiarism detection system.
Experimental research, on the other hand, focuses on establishing cause-and-effect relationships by manipulating variables and observing their impact under controlled conditions. In computer science research, experimental designs are frequently used to test algorithm performance, evaluate machine learning models, or compare computational techniques in controlled environments (Kitchenham & Charters, 2022; Amirzhanov et al., 2025). Within the context of plagiarism detection, experimental methods may be used to assess the accuracy of similarity detection algorithms or compare the effectiveness of different text processing techniques. However, experimental research alone does not provide a comprehensive methodological framework for the design, development, and implementation of a full-scale software system intended for institutional deployment (Pressman & Maxim, 2022; Sommerville, 2023).
Survey research is another widely used research approach that focuses on collecting quantitative or qualitative data from a population using structured instruments such as questionnaires or interviews. Surveys are particularly useful for gathering user opinions, requirements, and perceptions regarding system functionality and usability. In this study, survey techniques may support the collection of requirements from lecturers and students regarding plagiarism detection practices and system expectations (Kitchenham & Charters, 2022; Pressman & Maxim, 2022). However, survey research primarily functions as a data collection strategy and does not provide a methodological framework for designing and constructing a technological artifact.
Given that the primary objective of this study is to design, develop, and evaluate a functional software artifact, Design Science Research (DSR) provides the most appropriate methodological foundation. Design Science Research focuses on the creation and rigorous evaluation of artifacts such as models, frameworks, algorithms, or software systems intended to solve identified real-world problems (Gregor & Hevner, 2023; Peffers et al., 2022). In information systems research, Design Science Research  is widely adopted because it combines practical problem solving with systematic academic evaluation. In this study, the identified problem involves the limitations associated with reliance on external plagiarism detection systems, including high subscription costs and potential data privacy concerns. To address this challenge, a locally hosted Plagiarism Detection System will be designed and developed as the primary research artifact.
The research will begin with a structured problem identification phase aimed at understanding the operational challenges associated with existing plagiarism detection practices. According to the Design Science Research process model, problem identification and motivation represent the foundational stage that guides artifact development (Peffers et al., 2022; Gregor & Hevner, 2023). During this stage, data will be collected through interviews with lecturers and examination of current plagiarism verification workflows within the institution. This investigation is expected to reveal key challenges such as reliance on external platforms, limited archival capabilities for student submissions, and potential exposure of academic work to third-party systems. These issues highlight the need for a locally managed plagiarism detection solution.
Following the identification of the problem, clear solution objectives will be defined to guide the development of the proposed system. Design Science Research emphasizes that artifacts must be developed with clearly defined objectives that directly address the identified problem (Gregor & Hevner, 2023; Peffers et al., 2022). The primary objective of the proposed system will be to develop a web-based plagiarism detection platform capable of automatically comparing submitted documents with previously stored assignments in a centralized institutional repository. The system will also aim to ensure secure storage of academic documents, improve detection accuracy using modern text similarity techniques, and provide detailed similarity reports that assist lecturers in evaluating potential plagiarism cases.
The design and development phase will involve translating the identified requirements into a functional system architecture. Within the Design Science Research framework, artifact construction must follow systematic software engineering principles to ensure reliability and reproducibility (Pressman & Maxim, 2022; Sommerville, 2023). During this stage, functional and non-functional requirements will be documented and transformed into system models such as use case diagrams, system architecture diagrams, and database schemas. The system will be implemented as a web-based application consisting of modules for document submission, text preprocessing, similarity analysis, and report generation. A centralized repository will store all uploaded documents, enabling the system to compare new submissions with previously archived assignments.
At the core of the system will be a similarity detection engine responsible for identifying overlapping or semantically related text between documents. The engine will utilize techniques such as text fingerprinting, n-gram analysis, and semantic similarity detection to identify both direct copying and paraphrased forms of plagiarism. Combining lexical and semantic analysis improves the ability of plagiarism detection systems to detect subtle forms of text reuse that traditional string-matching methods may fail to capture (Alvi et al., 2021; Setu et al., 2025). This design ensures that the developed system can effectively identify plagiarism while maintaining full control of student submissions within the institutional infrastructure.
After development, a comprehensive evaluation phase will be conducted to determine the effectiveness and usability of the system. Evaluation is a critical component of Design Science Research, as artifacts must be rigorously assessed to determine their utility and performance in addressing the identified problem (Peffers et al., 2022; Gregor & Hevner, 2023). The evaluation will focus on criteria such as system usability, detection accuracy, functional correctness, and overall user satisfaction. Usability and satisfaction will be assessed using structured questionnaires administered to lecturers, while technical verification will be performed using testing techniques including unit testing, integration testing, and system testing to ensure that all system components function correctly (Pressman & Maxim, 2022; Sommerville, 2023).
Upon completion of the evaluation phase, the developed system will be formally demonstrated and communicated to an academic evaluation panel. In accordance with the communication stage of the Design Science Research methodology, the demonstration will present the system architecture, operational workflow, and core functionalities in a structured manner (Peffers et al., 2022; Gregor & Hevner, 2023). During the presentation, the panel will observe how the system processes document submissions, performs similarity analysis, and generates plagiarism reports. This demonstration will allow evaluators to assess the practical applicability and effectiveness of the proposed solution in supporting academic integrity management.
The Design Science Research methodology is therefore appropriate for this study because the primary objective is to design, implement, and rigorously evaluate a technological artifact that addresses a real-world problem. By applying the principles of Design Science Research , this research will produce a functional Plagiarism Detection System that improves plagiarism management while ensuring data privacy, institutional control of academic documents, and enhanced support for lecturers in maintaining academic integrity (Peffers et al., 2022; Sommerville, 2023).

3.2.2 Sampling
Sampling is the process of selecting a manageable subset from the target population to participate in a study, ensuring that the sample represents the characteristics and experiences of the broader population (Babbie, 2021; Taherdoost, 2022). The sampling frame defines the concrete list from which the sample is drawn and must closely align with the target population to minimize coverage error (Groves et al., 2021). For this study, the student sampling frame will be derived from Meru University’s 2025/2026 enrollment records filtered to courses requiring written submissions. Institutional records allow linkage—via anonymized study IDs—between survey responses and system logs for validation analyses, and including program and year variables supports accurate stratified sampling (Wang & Jiang, 2022; Sozon et al., 2024). The faculty frame will be derived from staff directories and course assignment lists to identify those actively supervising or grading written work, while administrative and technical staff frames will be obtained from IT and registry directories to capture personnel responsible for LMS administration and integrity processes. All frames will be validated against current schedules, discrepancies documented, and adjustments logged to maintain transparency and reduce coverage error (Groves et al., 2021; Sozon et al., 2024; Wang & Jiang, 2022).
This study employs a hybrid sampling strategy, combining census, stratified random, and purposive sampling. Small, critical groups, such as all supervisors and key LMS administrators, will be included using census sampling to ensure no essential perspectives are missed. Stratified random sampling will be used for students to ensure proportional representation across year and program, capturing heterogeneity in submission types and plagiarism risk, while purposive sampling will select faculty with heavy assessment loads to obtain relevant insights (Mireku, 2023; Taherdoost, 2022; Babbie, 2021). Within student strata, simple random sampling will be applied using seeded scripts on the validated enrollment frame to ensure reproducibility, with selection lists and seeds archived for auditability. Purposive criteria for faculty, such as minimum supervised projects, will be documented to maintain transparency. This combination preserves both statistical validity and the inclusion of specialized expertise.
To address nonresponse, the study will implement follow-ups including reminder emails and brief telephone prompts. Oversampling will compensate for potential dropouts, and nonresponse bias will be assessed by comparing respondents and nonrespondents on frame variables, with weighting adjustments applied as necessary. All weighting procedures and sensitivity analyses will be reported in appendices to maintain transparency and credibility (Kumar, 2023; Taherdoost, 2022; Mireku, 2023; Groves et al., 2021).
Sample size determination is based on clearly stated assumptions to ensure reproducibility. The target student population is N = 1,200, with a margin of error e = 0.05 and a conservative proportion p = 0.5, using a 95% confidence level (Z = 1.96). Cochran’s formula is applied as the baseline for estimating sample size for proportions, and a finite population correction is used to adjust for the known population size. As a cross-check, Slovin’s formula is also applied to confirm the sample size estimate. Based on these approaches, the student sample size will be approximately 291–300. Census inclusion of supervisors and key administrators, along with purposive selection of faculty, ensures that the study captures critical perspectives while maintaining statistical reliability. Ethical safeguards—including voluntary participation, informed consent, anonymization, and institutional oversight—will guide all sampling procedures (Kumar, 2023; Taherdoost, 2022; Groves et al., 2021; Sozon et al., 2024).

3.5 System Architecture And Development Tools
3.5.1 System Architecture 
The proposed plagiarism detection system will adopt a structured three-tier architecture comprising the presentation layer, application layer, and database layer. The three-tier architectural model is widely used in web-based information systems due to its ability to promote modularity, maintainability, scalability, and security (Pressman & Maxim, 2021). This architectural approach supports the separation of concerns by dividing system functionality into distinct but interrelated components, thereby improving system organization and structural clarity and allowing for the seamless integration of text processing modules (Sommerville, 2022).
The Presentation Layer, also referred to as the User Interface Layer, will manage all interactions between users and the plagiarism detection system. This layer will provide graphical interfaces through which students, lecturers, and system administrators will access the functionalities of the system. Through this interface, users will be able to register accounts, log into the system, upload academic documents for plagiarism analysis, and view generated similarity reports. The Presentation Layer will be implemented using HTML (HyperText Markup Language) to define the structural components of the web pages, CSS (Cascading Style Sheets) to control visual styling, layout organization, and readability, and JavaScript to provide client-side interactivity and dynamic user experience. JavaScript will also support preliminary input validation before information is transmitted to the server for processing. For example, when a student uploads a document for analysis, JavaScript will verify the file format and size before sending the request to the backend server. This improves usability while ensuring that final processing authority remains within the server-side logic. Users will access the system using standard web browsers such as Google Chrome, Mozilla Firefox, or Microsoft Edge, which provide compatibility with modern web technologies and support developer tools used during debugging and testing (Dix et al., 2021).
The Application Layer, commonly referred to as the Business Logic Layer, will contain the core processing mechanisms and operational rules of the plagiarism detection system. This layer will serve as the intermediary between the user interface and the database by receiving user requests, processing those requests according to defined system rules, and retrieving or storing relevant data within the database layer. The Application Layer will be implemented using the Python programming language together with the Django web framework. Django provides a secure and scalable environment for server-side processing and follows the Model–View–Template (MVT) architectural pattern, which separates data models, system logic, and interface rendering into structured components. This design approach enhances maintainability by allowing developers to modify individual components without affecting the entire system architecture (Pressman & Maxim, 2021).
Within the Application Layer, the plagiarism detection process will be performed through several functional modules. The document submission module will handle the secure uploading of assignments, essays, and other academic documents submitted by users. Uploaded documents will undergo preliminary validation before being forwarded for analysis. The text preprocessing module will then prepare the textual data for analysis through natural language processing techniques such as tokenization, stop-word removal, and normalization. These processes remove unnecessary words and standardize text structures, thereby improving the efficiency and accuracy of similarity detection algorithms (Jurafsky & Martin, 2023).
The similarity detection module will function as the analytical core of the system. This module will compare the submitted document against documents stored in the institutional repository to identify potential similarities. The system will utilize Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) vectorization combined with cosine similarity measurements to determine the similarity between documents based on word distribution patterns. These techniques are widely used within information retrieval and text mining applications for document comparison (Manning et al., 2022). In addition to lexical similarity detection, semantic similarity methods will also be used to capture contextual relationships between words using vector representations. This capability allows the system to detect advanced forms of plagiarism such as paraphrasing and structural similarity where direct word matching may not reveal copying behavior (Jurafsky & Martin, 2023). The integration of both lexical and semantic analysis techniques improves the effectiveness and reliability of plagiarism detection within the system (Amirzhanov et al., 2023).
The report generation module will compile the results of the similarity analysis into a structured plagiarism report that can be easily interpreted by lecturers and administrators. The report will present similarity percentages, highlight matching sections within the submitted document, and reference potential source documents identified within the repository. Providing clear and interpretable plagiarism reports enables educators to evaluate academic integrity violations accurately and make informed academic decisions (Arabi & Akbari, 2022).
The Database Layer will be responsible for persistent storage, management, and retrieval of system data. This layer will maintain structured records including user accounts, uploaded documents, processed textual content, and historical similarity analysis results. The database component will be implemented using PostgreSQL, an open-source relational database management system designed to manage large datasets efficiently while maintaining relational integrity. PostgreSQL will store information within normalized tables connected through primary and foreign key relationships to ensure referential integrity and consistency of stored data (Silberschatz, Korth, & Sudarshan, 2021). By isolating data storage within a dedicated layer, the system improves security, protects institutional academic documents, and simplifies maintenance procedures while enabling efficient document comparisons within the plagiarism repository.
The adoption of a three-tier architecture is justified by several established software engineering principles. First, it promotes separation of concerns by ensuring that interface design, system processing logic, and data storage operations are managed independently. Second, the architecture supports loose coupling between layers, meaning that modifications made within one layer such as updates to similarity detection algorithms will not significantly affect other layers of the system. Third, the architecture improves scalability, allowing the system to accommodate increasing numbers of document submissions as institutional usage grows. Fourth, the layered structure enhances maintainability and testability because each component can be independently developed, tested, and improved. Finally, system security is strengthened by restricting direct database access and enforcing authentication and validation mechanisms within the application layer (Pressman & Maxim, 2021). Overall, the three-tier architecture provides a robust and modular foundation for the proposed plagiarism detection system and aligns with best practices in web-based software engineering (Sommerville, 2022).

3.6 Data Analysis Methods
The quantitative data collected through structured questionnaires will be analyzed using descriptive statistical techniques. Descriptive statistics will be appropriate for this study because the objective is to summarize and describe patterns in respondents’ opinions regarding plagiarism awareness, current plagiarism detection practices, and expectations for the proposed plagiarism detection system rather than to test complex statistical hypotheses (Saunders, Lewis, & Thornhill, 2023). The analysis will involve transforming raw questionnaire responses into numerical values to facilitate systematic computation, comparison, and interpretation of the collected data.
All questionnaire responses will first be coded into numerical form to enable statistical processing. Each response category will be assigned a numerical value depending on the nature of the question. For instance, binary responses such as Yes or No will be coded numerically, while multiple-choice responses such as Strongly Agree, Agree, Neutral, Disagree, and Strongly Disagree will be assigned sequential values for analysis. Coding the data in this manner enables the researcher to organize responses in a structured format suitable for statistical analysis (Creswell & Creswell, 2023).
After coding the responses, frequencies will be calculated for each questionnaire item to determine how many respondents selected each response category. Percentages will then be computed to determine the proportion of respondents expressing particular opinions regarding plagiarism awareness, current plagiarism detection systems, and desired system features. Percentages will be calculated using the standard formula:
Percentage = (Frequency / Total Responses) × 100
For example, if 60 students complete the questionnaire and 45 indicate that plagiarism detection systems help discourage academic dishonesty, the percentage will be calculated as:
(45 / 60) × 100 = 75%
This result would indicate that 75% of respondents believe that plagiarism detection systems discourage academic dishonesty, thereby providing quantitative justification for the development of an institutional plagiarism detection system.
Similarly, if 40 out of 60 respondents indicate that the current plagiarism detection systems are expensive or difficult to access, the percentage will be calculated as:
(40 / 60) × 100 = 66.7%
This finding would demonstrate that 66.7% of respondents experience challenges with existing plagiarism detection tools, supporting the need for a locally hosted plagiarism detection system within the university.
The quantitative findings will be presented in tabular form to ensure clarity and facilitate comparison of response categories. Additionally, bar charts and pie charts will be used to visually represent frequency distributions and percentage comparisons, which improves the interpretability of survey findings and supports evidence-based decision making (Taherdoost, 2022). The use of descriptive statistical techniques will therefore provide clear summaries of user opinions that will guide the identification of system requirements for the proposed plagiarism detection system.
Qualitative data collected through semi-structured interviews with lecturers and academic staff will be analyzed using thematic analysis. Thematic analysis is a qualitative data analysis method used to identify, organize, and interpret recurring patterns or themes within textual data (Braun & Clarke, 2022). This method is particularly suitable for analyzing interview responses because it allows the researcher to systematically interpret participants’ experiences, perceptions, and recommendations regarding plagiarism detection practices within the university.
The qualitative analysis process will begin with the transcription of interview responses recorded during the interviews. The researcher will then repeatedly review the transcripts to gain familiarity with the collected data. During this process, significant statements, recurring opinions, and key observations will be identified and assigned descriptive codes representing the main ideas expressed by participants (O’Leary, 2021).
Similar codes will then be grouped into broader categories representing common issues and perspectives shared by participants. These categories will subsequently be organized into higher-level themes reflecting the major challenges and expectations related to plagiarism detection systems within the institution. For example, interview responses such as “external plagiarism systems are expensive,” “students sometimes submit similar assignments,” and “there is no central repository for past assignments” may initially be coded as high system cost, repeated submissions, and lack of document repository. These codes may then be grouped into broader themes such as limitations of existing plagiarism detection systems and need for institutional document repositories.
The identified themes will provide qualitative insights into the operational challenges faced by lecturers when detecting plagiarism and will help refine the functional and non-functional requirements of the proposed plagiarism detection system. For example, the theme relating to lack of a centralized assignment repository may support the inclusion of a database that stores previously submitted assignments for future similarity comparison.
The integration of quantitative and qualitative findings will provide a comprehensive understanding of plagiarism detection challenges and user expectations within the university environment. Quantitative analysis will offer measurable evidence of user awareness levels, system usage patterns, and preferences for plagiarism detection tools, while qualitative analysis will provide deeper explanations of the underlying causes of plagiarism detection challenges. The combination of descriptive statistical analysis and thematic analysis therefore reflects a mixed-method data analysis approach that supports evidence-based system development (Creswell & Creswell, 2023). Collectively, these analytical procedures will ensure that the proposed plagiarism detection system is designed based on empirically derived user requirements and real institutional needs.
3.7 System Testing and Evaluation
System testing and evaluation are essential phases in the software development process that ensure the developed system performs according to its intended design and meets the specified requirements. System testing refers to the process of executing a system with the objective of identifying errors and verifying that the integrated components function correctly as a complete system (Patton, 2021). Evaluation, on the other hand, involves assessing the system’s effectiveness, usability, and performance in real-world scenarios to determine whether it satisfies user expectations and operational goals (Myers, Sandler, & Badgett, 2022). For the plagiarism detection system, testing will be conducted through multiple stages including unit testing, integration testing, system testing, and user acceptance testing to ensure reliability and accuracy of the developed system (Ammann & Offutt, 2022).
Unit testing is the process of verifying the functionality of the smallest testable parts of a software application independently to ensure that each component performs its intended function correctly (Ammann & Offutt, 2022). In the plagiarism detection system, unit testing will be performed on individual modules such as the user authentication module, document upload module, text preprocessing module, similarity detection module, and plagiarism report generation module. Each component will be tested separately to confirm that it processes inputs correctly and produces the expected outputs. For instance, the document upload module will be tested to verify that it accepts valid file formats and rejects unsupported formats. Similarly, the text preprocessing module will be tested to ensure that operations such as tokenization, stop-word removal, and normalization function correctly before documents are passed to the similarity detection module. Conducting unit testing allows developers to identify and correct defects at an early stage of development, thereby improving system reliability and reducing debugging complexity in later stages.
Integration testing refers to the process of combining individual software modules and testing them as a group to ensure that they interact correctly and exchange data properly (Lewis, Smith, & Williams, 2021). The objective of integration testing is to detect errors that may occur due to interactions between system components.In the plagiarism detection system, integration testing will evaluate how different modules work together once they are connected. For example, the interaction between the document upload module and the text preprocessing module will be tested to confirm that uploaded documents are successfully processed before similarity analysis begins. Additionally, the connection between the similarity detection engine and the database system will be tested to ensure that documents are stored and retrieved accurately for comparison. This testing phase ensures that the frontend interface, Django backend logic, and database layer operate seamlessly without communication failures.
System testing is conducted after integration testing and involves testing the complete and fully integrated software system to determine whether it meets the specified functional and non-functional requirements (Kaur & Sharma, 2023). This testing phase evaluates the overall behaviour of the system under real operating conditions.For the plagiarism detection system, system testing will focus on validating the system’s core functionalities. These include user registration, user authentication, document submission, similarity analysis, and generation of plagiarism reports. The system will also undergo performance testing to measure the time taken to analyze documents and generate similarity scores. Additionally, security testing will be conducted to ensure that uploaded documents are securely stored and protected from unauthorized access. These tests help confirm that the system performs efficiently and securely when handling multiple users and document submissions.
User Acceptance Testing (UAT) is the final stage of the testing process in which the system is evaluated by actual users to determine whether it meets their expectations and satisfies their operational needs (Garcia & Torres, 2021). This type of testing focuses on verifying that the system is usable, functional, and capable of solving the problem for which it was developed.In this project, user acceptance testing will involve students and lecturers who are potential users of the plagiarism detection system. Participants will be asked to perform tasks such as uploading documents, running plagiarism checks, and interpreting the similarity reports generated by the system. After completing these tasks, users will provide feedback through a questionnaire assessing aspects such as ease of use, clarity of the interface, system responsiveness, and the usefulness of the plagiarism reports.
The effectiveness of the plagiarism detection system will be evaluated using several criteria, including usability, accuracy, response time, and user satisfaction.Usability refers to the degree to which a system can be used efficiently and effectively by users to achieve their intended goals (ISO, 2021). In this system, usability will be assessed by examining how easily users can navigate the interface, upload documents, and understand the generated plagiarism reports.Accuracy refers to the ability of the plagiarism detection system to correctly identify similarities between documents and produce reliable similarity scores. Accuracy will be evaluated by testing the system with sample documents containing known levels of similarity and verifying whether the system detects the similarities correctly.Response time measures how quickly the system processes a document and generates a plagiarism report. A shorter response time indicates better system performance and improved user experience.User satisfaction will be measured using a Likert scale questionnaire, where users rate their level of agreement with statements regarding the system’s performance, usability, and effectiveness. The Likert scale is a widely used method for measuring user perceptions and attitudes in information system evaluation studies (Taherdoost, 2022).
Through the implementation of these testing strategies and evaluation criteria, the developed plagiarism detection system will be validated to ensure that it is reliable, efficient, and capable of assisting academic institutions in identifying textual similarity in submitted documents.

3.8 Ethical Considerations
Ethical considerations are fundamental in research involving human participants and digital information systems. Ethical research practice ensures that participants’ rights, privacy, and welfare are protected while maintaining the integrity and credibility of the research process (Israel, 2021). Since this study involves collecting information from students and lecturers through questionnaires and interviews and also involves the design and testing of a plagiarism detection system that processes academic documents, several ethical principles must be observed. These include informed consent, protection of participants, confidentiality and privacy, participant feedback and debriefing, responsible data handling and storage, and transparency and honesty in reporting research findings. These principles will guide the entire research process to ensure integrity, accountability, and professional responsibility (Israel, 2021).
Informed consent refers to the process through which research participants voluntarily agree to participate in a study after receiving adequate information about the research objectives, procedures, and potential risks or benefits (Wiles, 2022). In this study, participants will be clearly informed about the purpose of the research, which is to gather requirements for the development of a plagiarism detection system for the School of Computing and Informatics at Meru University of Science and Technology. Before completing the questionnaire or participating in interviews, participants will be provided with a brief explanation describing the objectives of the study, the nature of the information being collected, and how the collected data will be used. Participants will also be informed that their participation is voluntary and that they have the right to decline participation or withdraw from the study at any time without facing any consequences.
Protection of participants is an essential ethical requirement in research to ensure that individuals involved in the study are not exposed to harm, discomfort, or negative consequences resulting from their participation (Resnik, 2022). In this research, measures will be taken to ensure that participants are not subjected to academic, psychological, or professional risks. For instance, responses collected from students and lecturers will not be used to evaluate individual academic performance or professional competence. The study will focus only on understanding plagiarism detection practices and system requirements. Participants will therefore be able to express their opinions freely without fear that their responses might negatively affect their academic or professional status.
Confidentiality and privacy involve protecting the identity and personal information of research participants and ensuring that sensitive data is not disclosed to unauthorized individuals (Walliman, 2021). In this study, the identities of respondents who complete questionnaires or participate in interviews will remain confidential. No personal identifiers such as names, registration numbers, or email addresses will be included in the data analysis or final research report. Responses collected from questionnaires will be analyzed collectively, and interview responses will be summarized without linking them to specific individuals. Maintaining confidentiality encourages participants to provide honest and accurate responses while safeguarding their personal information.
Participant feedback and debriefing involve informing participants about the outcomes of the study and clarifying how their contributions helped achieve the research objectives (Hammersley & Traianou, 2021). After data collection is completed, participants will be given the opportunity to receive information about the general findings of the research if they wish. Additionally, during the interview sessions, participants will be allowed to ask questions regarding the study and clarify any issues related to the proposed plagiarism detection system. Providing feedback and debriefing helps promote transparency and strengthens trust between researchers and participants.
Responsible data handling and storage are essential for protecting research information from unauthorized access, loss, or misuse. Data protection practices require researchers to implement appropriate safeguards when collecting, storing, and processing research data (ENISA, 2022).In this study, all questionnaire responses and interview notes will be stored in secure digital files that are accessible only to the researcher. Electronic data will be protected using password-protected devices and secure storage systems. Any documents used during system testing will also be stored securely to prevent unauthorized access. These measures ensure that both participant responses and academic documents used for system development are protected throughout the research process.
Transparency and honesty in reporting refer to the ethical responsibility of researchers to present research findings accurately without fabrication, falsification, or misrepresentation (National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, 2021). In this study, the methods used for data collection, system design, and evaluation will be clearly documented. The results obtained from questionnaire s and interviews will be reported truthfully, and any limitations encountered during the research will be openly acknowledged. Maintaining transparency and honesty in reporting ensures that the study contributes credible knowledge toward improving plagiarism detection practices within the university.
Overall, adherence to these ethical principles ensures that the research is conducted responsibly while protecting the rights and welfare of all participants involved in the study.
3.9 Summary
This chapter has presented the research methodology adopted in the development of the locally hosted Plagiarism Detection System for the School of Computing and Informatics at Meru University of Science and Technology. The study employed a structured and systematic approach through Design Science Research (DSR) to ensure that the developed system was grounded in empirical evidence and explicitly aimed at creating a functional technological artifact to solve real-world problems, such as high subscription costs and data privacy concerns associated with external platforms.
 To ensure comprehensive requirements elicitation, the research adopted a mixed-method approach, integrating both quantitative and qualitative data collection techniques. Quantitative data was collected using structured questionnaires, detailed in Appendix A, which were administered to students to capture measurable patterns regarding plagiarism awareness, current assignment submission practices, and preferences for system features. Qualitative data was gathered through semi-structured interviews, outlined in Appendix B, conducted with lecturers and administrative staff to obtain in-depth insights into existing verification workflows, operational challenges, and required system functionalities.
 The target population comprised undergraduate and postgraduate students, faculty members, and administrative staff within the selected school to capture the full ecosystem of academic integrity management. Appropriate sampling techniques, specifically a hybrid strategy combining census, stratified random, and purposive sampling, were applied to ensure both statistical validity and the representation of specialized stakeholder expertise.
Data collected through the questionnaires was analyzed using descriptive statistics, including frequencies and percentages, and presented using tables, bar charts, and pie charts to support evidence-based decision making. Interview data was analyzed using thematic analysis to identify recurring themes such as the high cost of external tools, repeated submissions, and the lack of a centralized document repository. These empirically derived themes directly informed the system's design and architecture; for example, the identified thematic need for a centralized repository justified the implementation of a robust database layer using PostgreSQL to store past assignments for continuous similarity comparison. 
The chapter also described the system development approach, outlining the Agile Software Development Methodology, specifically the Scrum framework, which was adopted because its iterative nature accommodates evolving requirements for complex features like text preprocessing and similarity detection algorithms. The system architecture follows a three-tier model, separating the presentation, application, and database layers to enhance scalability and allow the seamless integration of natural language processing tools like NLTK and Scikit-learn, while utilizing GitHub for collaborative version control. System testing was systematically planned and conducted at multiple levels, including unit testing, integration testing, system testing, and user acceptance testing, to ensure functional correctness, reliable module interaction, and overall usability. Evaluation metrics included usability, detection accuracy, system response time, and user satisfaction, which will be measured using a Likert scale questionnaire. 
Finally, ethical considerations were rigorously addressed to ensure informed consent, protection of participants from academic risk, confidentiality, secure responsible data handling, and transparency throughout the research process. 
Overall, this methodology ensured that the proposed Plagiarism Detection System was designed based on empirically derived user requirements, tested systematically, and evaluated rigorously to effectively support academic integrity within the university environment.
